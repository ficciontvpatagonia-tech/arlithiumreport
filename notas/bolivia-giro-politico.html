New Government, New Rules: Bolivia Pivots Away From Lithium Protectionism

The Paz administration courts foreign partners and a US realignment as YLB targets just 3,600 tonnes this year — a fraction of what the world's largest resource could yield.

By J. Martineau · AR Lithium Report

For two decades, Bolivia treated lithium as a strategic resource too important to share: state-controlled, foreign participation minimised, industrialisation promised but perpetually postponed. That era is ending.

President Rodrigo Paz, who took office in November 2025 at the head of a centre-right, pro-business government, has signalled a decisive turn — closer ties with the United States, openness to foreign operators, and a willingness to renegotiate the terms under which Bolivia's brines get developed.

The gap to close

The distance between potential and reality remains enormous. Bolivia holds the world's largest lithium resource, with roughly 21% of the global total, yet state firm YLB expects to produce about 3,600 tonnes of carbonate this year — a few hundredths of what Chile extracts from a smaller salar next door.

The pending agreements with China's CBC and Russia's Uranium One, negotiated under the previous government and questioned during the campaign, now sit in a new light: the administration wants the investment but on terms it can defend publicly, after criticism that earlier contracts were negotiated with little transparency.

Risks on the ground

Political risk has not disappeared — episodes of unrest have already reminded investors that world-class assets are only as bankable as the institutions around them, and communities around Uyuni continue to press for guarantees on water and local benefit.

Still, the change is real: for the first time since the lithium boom began, no corner of the Triangle is closed for business. What Bolivia does with that opening will shape the region's supply map for decades.
