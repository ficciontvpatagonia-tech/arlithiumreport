Atacama Expansion Set to Lift Chilean Output to 67,300 Tonnes in 2026

SQM's ramp-up drove a 10.1% production rise in 2025, and the Codelco partnership locks the operation in through 2060. But Chile's lead over Argentina keeps narrowing.

By J. Martineau · AR Lithium Report

Chile remains the Lithium Triangle's heavyweight, and the Salar de Atacama its beating heart. Production rose an estimated 10.1% in 2025 to 64,100 tonnes, driven by SQM's continuing capacity expansion, and analysts forecast a further 4.9% rise to 67,300 tonnes in 2026.

Stability is the new story. The Codelco–SQM partnership approved this cycle secures the Atacama operation through 2060 under state-majority control, ending years of uncertainty about what would happen when SQM's lease expired in 2030.

The constraints are real

Growth in the Atacama is not unconstrained. Production quotas, water stewardship in the driest desert on Earth and standing commitments to indigenous communities all shape how many tonnes the salar can add — and the new venture's direct-extraction plans are designed, in part, to produce more lithium per litre of brine precisely because of those limits.

The regional race

The wider context is competitive: projections have Chile slipping from the world's second-largest producer toward fourth place by decade's end as Argentina and other producers scale faster. Chile's bet is that quality beats speed — the lowest costs in the industry, a locked-in institutional framework and a technology upgrade worth $3bn.

Whether that is enough to keep the crown in the Triangle is one of the defining questions of the next five years.
