Rio Tinto Locks In $1.175bn for Rincón as First Lithium Cargo Sails to Shanghai

Four development banks back the $2.5bn Salta project while the first 200 tonnes of carbonate leave Buenos Aires. Rio's Argentine lithium commitment now tops $3.9bn across five assets.

By J. Martineau · AR Lithium Report

Rio Tinto has secured a $1.175bn financing package for its Rincón lithium project in Salta, a deal that broadens the funding base of the $2.5bn operation and signals that development banks are willing to underwrite Argentina's lithium build-out at scale.

The package brings together the International Finance Corporation (IFC), IDB Invest, Export Finance Australia and the Japan Bank for International Cooperation (JBIC) — a roster that reads like a map of who wants secure lithium supply this decade: Washington-based multilaterals, Canberra and Tokyo.

"This financing package broadens the funding sources available for the Rincón project and supports the continued execution of our lithium growth pipeline," said Jérôme Pécresse, chief executive of Rio Tinto Aluminium & Lithium.

First cargo, long horizon

The announcement lands just as Rincón's first production leaves the country: 200 tonnes of lithium carbonate, packed into ten containers, shipped from the Port of Buenos Aires to Shanghai. It is a symbolic volume — but a milestone for a project designed to run for more than 40 years.

At full capacity, targeted for 2028, Rincón is expected to produce 60,000 tonnes a year of battery-grade lithium carbonate, which would make it one of the largest single lithium operations in the country.

The bigger Argentine bet

Rincón is one piece of a much larger position. Rio Tinto has now committed more than $3.9bn across five lithium assets in Argentina, a portfolio inherited in part through its acquisition of Arcadium Lithium: the producing Fénix operation and the Olaroz joint venture (Rio Tinto 66.5%, Toyota Tsusho 25%, JEMSE 8.5%), plus the Fénix 1B and Sal de Vida expansions under construction in Catamarca.

Taken together, the numbers make Rio Tinto the single biggest corporate bet on Argentine lithium — and the clearest test of whether the country's open-market model can deliver world-scale supply on schedule.
